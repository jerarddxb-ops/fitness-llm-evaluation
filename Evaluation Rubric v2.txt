Response Evaluation Rubric (V2)


Rubric V1 – Common Errors
	1	Confused weak vs misleading
	2	Didn’t treat myths as high severity
	3	Occasionally added personal expectations
	4	Error types were sometimes too vague
	5	Learning signals were too vague and generic

V2 Updates
	1	Updated evaluation principles (1, 4, 8)
	2	Updated error types
	3	Updated guidelines for learning signals



1. Evaluation Principles
	1	Correctness takes priority Responses that contain incorrect or misleading information should not be preferred, even if they are clear or well-written. Arguments that support myths are always worse than answers that are weak but factual.

	2	Use comparative reasoning Justifications must explain rankings by comparing responses, not evaluating them in isolation.

	3	Reference rubric criteria explicitly Each justification should reference at least two criteria (e.g., correctness, helpfulness, clarity, conciseness).

	4	Avoid personal preference bias Evaluate based on usefulness to a general user, not personal writing style or subjective preference.Focus only on what is given in the response.

	5	Prioritize user usefulness Select the response that would be most helpful to a typical user, even if it is not the most concise or stylistically polished.

	6	Handle trade-offs explicitly When responses have strengths in different areas (e.g., clarity vs completeness), explicitly identify and weigh these trade-offs.

	7	Conciseness is a secondary criterion Only use conciseness when correctness and helpfulness are comparable.

	8	Weak ≠ incorrect or misleading Always distinguish between:
	◦	Incorrect / misleading = factually wrong or implies a false conclusion
	◦	Weak = vague or incomplete

	9	Weak responses are preferred over misleading ones.



2. Evaluation Criteria

1. Correctness (Highest Priority)
Definition: Is the information factually accurate and free from misleading or incorrect claims?
Guide:
	•	High = accurate, no misleading information
	•	Medium = mostly correct, minor issues
	•	Low = incorrect or misleading

2. Helpfulness
Definition: Does the response directly and effectively answer the user’s question?
Guide:
	•	High = clear, actionable, fully answers
	•	Medium = partially helpful or incomplete
	•	Low = vague, off-topic, or unhelpful

3. Clarity
Definition: Is the response easy to understand and well-structured?
Guide:
	•	High = clear, well-organized
	•	Medium = understandable but slightly messy
	•	Low = confusing or poorly structured

4. Conciseness (Secondary)
Definition: Is the response appropriately detailed without unnecessary length?
Guide:
	•	High = efficient, no fluff
	•	Medium = slightly too long or too short
	•	Low = overly verbose or too brief



3. Confidence
	•	3 = High Confidence “I’m very sure this is correct”
	◦	clear winner
	◦	obvious trade-offs
	◦	low ambiguity
	•	2 = Medium Confidence “I think this is right, but not certain”
	◦	some trade-offs
	◦	close between responses
	•	1 = Low Confidence “This was genuinely difficult”
	◦	strong trade-offs
	◦	multiple valid answers
	◦	unclear best choice



4. Error Taxonomy
Use 1–2 error types per response, prioritizing the primary failure over secondary effects.

Factual Error
Factually incorrect information (always wrong). Includes false necessity/impossibility claims. Test: Is this EVER true? If not → false claim.

Overgeneralization
Applies something that is sometimes true as if it is always true. Scope error (e.g., “always”, “must”, “best”).

Misleading Causal Claim
Presents a cause-and-effect relationship (X → Y) that is incorrect, overstated, or not supported by evidence. Often occurs alongside overgeneralization.

Incomplete Answer
Correct, but fails to address key aspects of the question.

Lack of Guidance
Too vague or non-actionable to be useful, even if factually correct.



5. Learning Signals
Definition
A learning signal captures the primary failure pattern in the comparison and expresses it as a general, reusable rule.

Guidelines
	•	Focus on one primary insight
	•	Avoid generic quality statements
	•	Avoid multiple ideas in one signal
	•	Do not rely on domain-specific wording
	•	Convert the error type into a general rule

Signal Structure
Prefer responses that [DO X] rather than [DO Y]

Example
	•	Weak: “Prefer detailed and correct responses”
	•	Strong: “Prefer responses that distinguish between short-term mechanisms and long-term outcomes”



Summary
This rubric was refined through iterative evaluation, addressing:
	•	confusion between weak vs misleading responses
	•	handling of myths and causal reasoning
	•	clarity and precision of error classification
	•	specificity and usefulness of learning signals
