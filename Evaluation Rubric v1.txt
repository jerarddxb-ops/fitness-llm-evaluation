Response Evaluation Rubric (V1)



1. Evaluation Principles
	1	Correctness takes priority Responses that contain incorrect or misleading information should not be preferred, even if they are clear or well-written.

	2	Use comparative reasoning Justifications must explain the ranking by comparing responses, not evaluating them in isolation.

	3	Reference rubric criteria explicitly Each justification should reference at least two criteria (e.g., correctness, helpfulness, clarity, conciseness).

	4	Avoid personal preference bias Evaluations should be based on usefulness to a general user, not personal writing style or subjective preference.

	5	Prioritize user usefulness Select the response that would be most helpful to a typical user, even if it is not the most concise or stylistically polished.

	6	Handle trade-offs explicitly When responses have strengths in different areas (e.g., clarity vs completeness), explicitly identify and weigh these trade-offs in the justification.

	7	Conciseness is a secondary criterion Conciseness should only influence decisions when correctness and helpfulness are comparable.



2. Evaluation Criteria

1. Correctness (takes priority)
Definition: Is the information factually accurate and free from misleading or incorrect claims?
Guide:
	•	High = accurate, no misleading info
	•	Medium = mostly correct, minor issues
	•	Low = incorrect or misleading

2. Helpfulness
Definition: Does the response directly and effectively answer the user’s question?
Guide:
	•	High = clear, actionable, fully answers
	•	Medium = partially helpful or incomplete
	•	Low = vague, off-topic, or unhelpful

3. Clarity
Definition: Is the response easy to understand and well-structured?
Guide:
	•	High = clear, well-organized
	•	Medium = understandable but slightly messy
	•	Low = confusing or poorly structured

4. Conciseness
Definition: Is the response appropriately detailed without unnecessary length?
Guide:
	•	High = efficient, no fluff
	•	Medium = slightly too long/short
	•	Low = overly verbose or too brief



3. Confidence
	•	3 = High Confidence “I’m very sure this is correct”
	◦	clear winner
	◦	obvious trade-offs
	◦	low ambiguity
	•	2 = Medium Confidence “I think this is right, but not certain”
	◦	some trade-offs
	◦	close between two responses
	•	1 = Low Confidence “This was genuinely difficult”
	◦	strong trade-offs
	◦	multiple valid answers
	◦	unclear best choice



4. Error Taxonomy

Common Error Types:

Factual error
 → Incorrect or misleading information

Overgeneralization
 → Advice that is too broad or extreme (e.g., “cut carbs completely”)

Lack of specificity
 → Too vague to be useful

Incomplete answer
 → Misses key aspects of the question

Overconfidence
 → Sounds certain despite being wrong or weak

Verbosity
 → Unnecessarily long without adding value

Clarity issue
 → Difficult to understand or poorly structured

Off-topic / irrelevant
 → Does not directly address the question



5. Learning Signals

Learning signal: A brief statement describing what the model should learn from this comparison (i.e., which qualities to prefer or avoid in future responses). Should be more general than the Justification.

Example:
Prefer responses that explain underlying principles clearly over those that give oversimplified or extreme advice
